This thesis follows the evolution of a representation-centered research question in end-to-end autonomous driving. The project began with a simple hypothesis: replacing supervised visual backbones with self-supervised ones should improve planning even when the downstream head is lightweight. During the course of the project, JEPA-based planners rapidly improved standard mixed-city NAVSIM performance, shifting the most informative question away from pure in-distribution accuracy and toward robustness: \emph{where does self-supervised pre-training matter most, and why?}

The thesis therefore develops in three stages. First, it uses lightweight perception-free planning as the conceptual starting point for a representation-first view of end-to-end driving. Second, it introduces \emph{NAVSIM City Splits}, the first per-city train/test partitioning of NAVSIM across Boston, Pittsburgh, Las Vegas, and Singapore, and uses this benchmark to show that self-supervised backbones reduce zero-shot cross-city generalization gaps by an order of magnitude across LAW, TransFuser, and Latent TransFuser. Third, it tests whether the same representation-centric finding extends to generative planning by integrating SSL backbones into DiffusionDrive, including a camera-only variant, and then investigates \emph{why} SSL transfers better using Centered Kernel Alignment, linear probing for city classification, MMD, and effective-rank analysis. Across these stages, the central conclusion is consistent: the most distinctive value of self-supervised visual representations is not merely higher mixed-city benchmark performance, but improved robustness under geographic domain shift and a more city-invariant feature space.

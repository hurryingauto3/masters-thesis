End-to-end autonomous driving models trained on one city often fail when deployed in another due to visual domain shift --- differences in road geometry, vegetation, signage, and weather across geographic locations. Yet current benchmarks like NAVSIM aggregate performance across cities, masking this critical generalization failure. This thesis investigates whether the choice of visual representation is the key determinant of cross-city robustness.

We make four contributions. First, we introduce \emph{NAVSIM City Splits}, the first per-city train/test partitioning of the NAVSIM benchmark across Boston, Pittsburgh, Las Vegas, and Singapore, enabling systematic cross-city evaluation. Second, we conduct a comprehensive comparison of self-supervised learning (SSL) backbones --- I-JEPA, DINOv2, and MAE --- against supervised ImageNet ResNet features, within both the TransFuser and LAW (Latent World Model) planning architectures. Our results show that SSL representations reduce cross-city generalization gaps by an order of magnitude, demonstrating that cross-city failure is fundamentally a \emph{representation} problem rather than a planning architecture problem. Third, we propose \emph{JEPA-Diffusion}, a novel camera-only planner that combines a frozen I-JEPA ViT encoder with a truncated diffusion planning head derived from DiffusionDrive, targeting competitive EPDMS performance at real-time inference speeds without requiring LiDAR. Fourth, we compare generative (diffusion) and regression planning heads under cross-city transfer to determine whether the planning head architecture independently affects generalization. Our findings provide practical guidance for building geographically robust autonomous driving systems using self-supervised visual representations.
